# Honesty Calibration: What You Can and Can't Claim

One of the trickiest parts of entering the AI/ML space from a traditional psychology background is figuring out how to talk about your skills honestly. Overselling leads to situations where you're expected to deliver things you can't. Underselling means you don't get the opportunities you've genuinely prepared for. Neither serves you well on the job market, in collaborations, or in grant applications.

This guide is designed to help you calibrate. After working through this crash course, you will have real, substantive knowledge -- but it has boundaries. Knowing exactly where those boundaries are is itself a sign of expertise. The most credible researchers are the ones who can articulate precisely what they know and what they're still learning.

---

## After Completing This Course, You CAN Say...

These are honest, defensible claims you can make in interviews, cover letters, grant applications, and conversations with collaborators. Each one reflects genuine knowledge you've built.

1. **"I have conceptual fluency with AI/ML terminology and methods."**
   You can follow technical conversations, read ML-oriented papers without getting lost in jargon, and ask informed questions. That fluency is real and valuable.

2. **"I've used AI tools to perform text classification and qualitative coding comparisons."**
   You have hands-on experience using AI tools for supervised classification tasks and comparing manual versus AI-assisted qualitative coding workflows. You did this yourself through guided exercises, not just in the abstract.

3. **"I understand how NLP, predictive modeling, and digital phenotyping apply to psychological research."**
   You can articulate specific use cases, identify which methods map onto which research questions, and evaluate whether a given application makes sense for a study.

4. **"I can critically evaluate AI/ML research papers in my field."**
   You know what to look for: appropriate validation strategies, overfitting risks, sample size considerations, interpretability trade-offs, and whether the claims match the methods.

5. **"I have a concrete research vision for integrating computational methods into my program of work."**
   You've thought carefully about where ML and NLP fit into body image research, adolescent development, and mother-daughter dynamics -- not as a gimmick, but as a genuine methodological extension.

6. **"I understand the ethical considerations of AI/ML with youth and vulnerable populations."**
   You can speak to algorithmic bias, data privacy, consent challenges with minors, and the risks of deploying predictive tools in sensitive clinical contexts.

7. **"I can identify and initiate collaboration opportunities with computational researchers."**
   You know enough to have productive conversations with data scientists and ML engineers. You can describe what you need, understand what they offer, and contribute meaningfully to interdisciplinary teams.

8. **"I know the landscape of AI/ML applications in eating disorder and body image research."**
   You've surveyed the literature, understand what's been tried, what's promising, and where the gaps are. That domain-specific knowledge is something most ML researchers don't have.

9. **"I've used AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude as research aids for brainstorming, literature synthesis, and drafting."**
   You have practical experience using large language models as part of your workflow, and you understand both their utility and their limitations.

10. **"I'm actively building computational skills as part of a deliberate research development plan."**
    This frames your trajectory honestly. You're not pretending to be somewhere you're not -- you're showing momentum and intention.

## After Completing This Course, You Should NOT Say...

These claims would misrepresent your current skill level. Saying them in a professional context could damage your credibility if someone probes further. Avoiding them is not a weakness -- it's integrity.

1. **"I'm an expert in machine learning."**
   Expertise implies years of deep technical work, original methodological contributions, or extensive applied experience. You're an informed beginner with strong domain knowledge. That's a different thing, and it's still valuable.

2. **"I have extensive experience with deep learning or neural networks."**
   You understand what these are conceptually, but you haven't built transformer models or trained convolutional networks. Don't imply otherwise.

3. **"I can independently build NLP pipelines from scratch."**
   You understand the components of an NLP workflow and can contribute to one, but designing and implementing a full pipeline for research-grade text analysis requires more specialized training.

4. **"I'm proficient in Python for data science."**
   Your technical foundation is in R. You may have some Python exposure, but proficiency implies fluency you haven't yet developed. If Python comes up, say you're R-based and learning.

5. **"I've published work using AI/ML methods."**
   Unless you have by the time you're making this claim, don't say it. You can say you have work in development or that you're designing studies that incorporate these methods.

6. **"I can teach a graduate course on machine learning."**
   You could give a guest lecture or lead a journal club discussion. Teaching a full course requires depth you're still building.

7. **"I have production-level ML experience."**
   Production-level implies deploying models in real-world systems with monitoring, scaling, and maintenance. Your experience is research-oriented and educational, which is the appropriate context for your career stage.

## How to Gracefully Handle the Gap

The space between what you know and what someone might expect you to know will come up. Here are four realistic scenarios with language you can adapt.

### Scenario 1: The Job Interview

**They ask:** "What's your experience with machine learning?"

**You say:** "I've completed focused training in AI/ML methods with direct application to my research area. I've compared manual and AI-assisted qualitative coding, worked through guided paper analysis, and used AI tools for text classification exercises, and I have a strong conceptual grasp of NLP, digital phenotyping, and how these tools apply to adolescent psychology. My strength is that I combine genuine computational literacy with deep domain expertise in body image and developmental research -- I can bridge the gap between the clinical questions and the technical methods. I'm also continuing to build these skills through [specific next steps you've planned]."

This answer is honest, specific, and forward-looking. It positions your domain expertise as the asset it is.

### Scenario 2: The Collaborator Meeting

**They ask:** "Can you run the NLP analysis for our project?"

**You say:** "I can contribute meaningfully to the design and interpretation side -- I understand the method well enough to help us define the right approach, select appropriate features, and evaluate whether the results make sense clinically. For the implementation, I'd want to either partner with someone who has deeper technical NLP experience or invest time in building that specific skill before we start. I'd rather be upfront about that now than run into problems mid-project."

Transparency like this builds trust. Collaborators would much rather hear this than discover limitations after work has begun.

### Scenario 3: The Grant Review

**A reviewer writes:** "The applicant's computational expertise is not well established. It's unclear whether the team can execute the proposed NLP analyses."

**You respond (in your resubmission):** "The PI has completed structured training in AI/ML methods applied to psychological research, including hands-on model building in R and critical evaluation of computational approaches in the eating disorder literature. The project team includes [computational collaborator] who brings [specific technical expertise]. This collaboration model mirrors the mentored research development framework of K-award mechanisms, where the PI's growing computational fluency is paired with established technical mentorship. The PI's unique contribution is domain expertise in [your specific area] that ensures the computational methods are applied to clinically meaningful questions."

This reframes the critique. You're not hiding a gap -- you're showing a deliberate, well-supported plan.

### Scenario 4: The Curious Colleague

**They ask:** "I heard you know about ML -- can you help me with my project?"

**You say:** "I'd be happy to talk it through with you. I can probably help you think about whether ML is the right approach for your question, what methods might apply, and what to watch out for. If it turns out you need someone to actually build the pipeline, I can help you think about who to reach out to or what resources to look into. Let's start with what you're trying to do."

This is generous without overcommitting. You're offering what you genuinely can provide -- conceptual guidance and informed direction -- without pretending to be a technical consultant.

## The Growth Trajectory Frame

Here is the most important reframe in this entire guide: you are not pretending to be something you're not. You are a domain expert who is strategically expanding into computational methods. That is a recognized, respected career development arc in academic psychology.

The NIH K-award mechanism is built on exactly this premise -- that researchers at certain career stages benefit from protected time to develop new methodological skills under mentorship. Your trajectory mirrors that logic, whether or not you're on a K-award. You have deep expertise in your substantive area, and you're deliberately building the technical skills to ask your research questions in new ways.

When you frame your computational development this way, it signals three things to hiring committees, reviewers, and collaborators:

- **Self-awareness.** You know where you are and where you're going. That's more trustworthy than vague claims of expertise.
- **Strategic thinking.** You're not chasing a trend. You've identified specific ways that AI/ML methods can advance your existing research program.
- **Growth potential.** You're early in a trajectory that will compound. Hiring you now means getting someone whose skills are actively expanding, not static.

The researchers who will have the most impact in computational psychology over the next decade are not the ones who are already technical experts. They are the ones -- like you -- who combine real domain knowledge with genuine computational literacy and the motivation to keep learning.
