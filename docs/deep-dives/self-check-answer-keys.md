## Week 1 Self-Check Answers

The Week 1 self-check is in [Week 1 Guide](../week-1/index.md), Section: Self-Check.

### 1. Explain AI vs. ML using the nested-circles analogy

Key points:

- AI is the broadest circle: any intelligent computer system
- ML is a subset: systems that learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed
- Deep learning is a subset of ML: multi-layered neural networks that power things like ChatGPT
- When psychologists say "AI/ML" they usually mean machine learning specifically
- Explaining this clearly to a colleague shows you understand the relationship, not just the terms

### 2. Supervised learning — explain it and name a psychology method

Key points:

- Supervised learning: the model learns from labeled data (input + correct answer). It learns the mapping from predictors to outcome
- Psychology equivalent: logistic regression (you provide the outcome, the model learns what predicts it). Also: any regression, discriminant function analysis
- Unsupervised learning is the contrast: model finds structure without labels (like factor analysis or cluster analysis)

### 3. 30-second NLP pitch for psychology researchers

Key points:

- NLP = AI methods for understanding human language
- Psychology generates enormous text data: therapy transcripts, open-ended survey responses, social media posts, conversation recordings
- NLP scales qualitative analysis — it doesn't replace your interpretive expertise, it amplifies your capacity
- Specific techniques: sentiment analysis, topic modeling, text classification (what you did with the Mia interview)

### 4. One specific AI/ML extension of your research

Key points:

- The strongest answers connect a specific method to a specific research question
- Weak: "I could use AI for my research." Strong: "NLP could classify body image language in my mother-daughter transcripts, scaling my manual coding to hundreds of conversations"
- Name the method, the data, and the question it would address

### 5. Overfitting analogy for a psychology grad student

Key points:

- Overfitting occurs when a model memorizes noise in the training data and performs poorly on new data
- Psychology analogy: running a regression with 30 predictors on 40 participants — you get an amazing R² that means nothing because you've capitalized on chance. Or: developing a screening measure on one specific sample that fails to replicate
- Solution: evaluate on held-out data the model never saw during training (cross-validation)

## Week 2 Self-Check Answers

The Week 2 self-check is in [Week 2 Guide](../week-2/index.md), Section: Self-Check.

### 1. Conference NLP pitch — two specific examples

Key points:

- Give two concrete applications relevant to your area, not generic descriptions
- Examples: automated coding of conversation transcripts (replacing or supplementing manual coding), sentiment analysis of social media body image content at scale, topic modeling across therapy session transcripts
- The person asking wants to understand what NLP would actually *do* for psychology research — make it tangible

### 2. Explanation vs. prediction — respond to a colleague

Key points:

- Their approach (explanation) asks "which factors are associated with depression?" — regression coefficients, p-values, effect sizes
- A prediction approach asks "can we forecast *which specific individuals* will develop depression based on their data?"
- Both matter: explanation identifies intervention targets (what to change), prediction identifies who needs intervention (where to direct resources)
- Frame it as complementary, not competing — ML extends the questions your colleague is already asking

### 3. The 97% accuracy question — what to ask first

Key points:

- Ask about the base rate: how common is the condition in the sample?
- If only 3% of the sample has an eating disorder, a model that always predicts "no ED" gets 97% accuracy — but catches zero cases
- For rare but serious conditions, sensitivity (recall) matters more than overall accuracy
- This is the class imbalance problem — and it's pervasive in clinical screening research

### 4. Ethical concerns interview answer

Key points:

- Be specific to your research area and population — generic answers about "bias" are weaker than specific concerns about your data and participants
- Strong topics: consent/assent for minors, algorithmic bias against diverse body types and cultural groups, privacy of digital phenotyping data, clinical obligations when models flag risk
- The strongest answers go beyond naming concerns to articulating plans for addressing them
- Position ethics as integrated into your research design, not a separate checkbox

## How to Use These Answer Keys

1. **Score yourself honestly.** For each question, ask: did I hit the key points?
2. **Revisit weak spots.** Go back to the relevant week's guide for topics you missed.
3. **Do not memorize these answers.** The goal is understanding, not recall. If you can explain these concepts to a colleague in your own words, you have mastered the material.
